Understanding trauma

PTSD & Complex PTSD

What they are, how they differ, and why understanding the difference can change everything.

Understanding the difference between PTSD and Complex PTSD matters, because while they are related, they are not the same. Both involve trauma — but the type, the duration, and the way that trauma reshapes us can be very different. Knowing which is which is often the first step out of years of believing something is wrong with you.

PTSD — Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder

PTSD typically develops after a single traumatic event, or a short period of intense trauma. Examples include:

  • Serious accidents
  • Violent assaults
  • Military combat
  • Natural disasters
  • Witnessing death or severe injury
  • Life-threatening medical emergencies

After the event, the brain struggles to fully process what happened. The traumatic memory stays “unresolved,” and the nervous system keeps reacting as if the danger were still present. Common symptoms include:

  • Flashbacks or intrusive memories
  • Nightmares
  • Avoiding reminders of the trauma
  • Hyper-alertness, or being easily startled
  • Anxiety and disturbed sleep

With the right support, many people recover significantly from PTSD.

Complex PTSD — when trauma is prolonged

CPTSD usually develops from prolonged or repeated trauma over time, especially in situations where a person feels trapped, powerless, or unable to escape. Examples include:

  • Childhood abuse or neglect
  • Long-term domestic violence
  • Human trafficking or captivity
  • Long periods of extreme stress or danger
  • Repeated traumatic exposure (as sometimes seen in emergency services, military, or frontline roles)

Instead of one traumatic memory to resolve, the person's entire nervous system becomes shaped by long-term survival mode. So alongside the classic PTSD symptoms, CPTSD brings deeper emotional and psychological effects:

  • Difficulty regulating emotions — flooded, or numb
  • Chronic feelings of shame or worthlessness
  • Difficulty trusting others or forming close relationships
  • Feeling detached from yourself or the world
  • Persistent anxiety or emotional numbness
  • A deeply altered sense of who you are

These deeper effects are so consistent that in 2018 the World Health Organization formally recognised Complex PTSD as its own diagnosis in the ICD-11 — defined as the core of PTSD plus three “disturbances in self-organisation”: trouble regulating emotion, a negative sense of self, and difficulty in relationships.

The key difference

One wound, or a long climate

PTSD

A wound from a single event

The nervous system gets caught in one moment of overwhelming threat, and keeps replaying it.

COMPLEX PTSD

The result of living in trauma

There is no single memory to resolve; survival itself became the shape of the nervous system, often from childhood.

PTSD is often a wound from a single traumatic event.

CPTSD is the result of living in trauma over time.

Both are real. Both are serious. Both deserve understanding and support.

What is actually happening in the brain and body

None of this is imagined, and none of it is weakness. A handful of well-studied changes explain almost everything a survivor lives with:

  • The amygdala — the smoke alarm. The brain's threat detector becomes hair-trigger and stays switched on. It cannot tell the difference between then and now.
  • The hippocampus — the timekeeper, offline. Under stress hormones, the part that stamps a memory as “past, over” is suppressed — so the memory returns feeling like it is happening now.
  • The thinking brain, offline. Under threat the reasoning brain goes quiet. This is why you cannot simply think your way out of a flashback — it is not a failure of will.
  • Speechless terror. Brain imaging shows the speech centre can deactivate during trauma. Some of it was never stored in words in the first place — which is why it is so hard to talk about.
  • The body keeps the score. The stress-hormone system stays dysregulated, which over years can affect sleep, digestion, immunity, pain, and energy.

Because trauma lives in these fast, wordless, body-based systems — and not only in the thinking, speaking brain — talking about it can only take you so far. The body learned it; the body has to be part of how it heals. (This is the heart of the somatic, nature- and water-based approach — read more here.)

Why awareness matters

Nothing is wrong with you. Something happened to you.

Many people with CPTSD spend years believing they are broken. In reality, their brain and nervous system adapted — brilliantly — to survive extremely difficult circumstances. Understanding the difference between PTSD and CPTSD removes the stigma, and lets people find the right kind of support.

Trauma is not a weakness. It is something the human mind endured in order to survive. And recovery is possible.
