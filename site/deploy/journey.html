My story

The long way home to myself.

I spent my life in the water — healing wild places, while quietly carrying wounds of my own. This is how I found my way back, and why I do this work.

For most of my life, two things were true at once. On the surface: a marine and freshwater scientist, a diver, a conservationist — capable, driven, at home in the sea. Underneath: a woman shaped by childhood trauma, carrying anger, shame, and a quiet certainty that something in me was wrong. It took me a long time to understand that the two were connected — and even longer to find the way through. This is that story.

The beginning

A childhood that shaped a nervous system

The hardest part came first, and it stayed. Growing up with fear and instability, my body learned early that the world was not safe. The symptoms followed me through childhood, my youth, and well into adulthood — the flooding emotions, the harsh inner voice, the trouble trusting and being close. I didn't have a name for it then. I only knew I was struggling with things that seemed to come easily to everyone else.

A first refuge

Nature was the first place I felt safe

Before I had any words for what was happening inside me, I had the water and the wild. Nature asked nothing of me. It didn't judge, didn't leave, didn't turn frightening. In it, my body could rest in a way it couldn't anywhere else. I didn't know yet that this was regulation. I only knew it was the one place I could breathe.

The years in the water

A life given to the ocean — and real success

That refuge became a calling. I built a career as a marine and freshwater ecologist and conservation scientist, spending some ten years in and under the water — studying it, protecting it, teaching others to love it. By every outward measure, I was thriving. The wild had given me a life. And I gave it back everything I had.

The dissonance

Success outside, struggle within

And yet. However well my professional life went, my inner life kept telling a different story — anger that surprised me, a low sense of my own worth, shame I couldn't reason away, and relationships that kept getting caught on the same painful hooks. I carried a fear I rarely said out loud: that I might not be able to become a mother, to build the family and closeness I longed for, because of what I was still carrying. The gap between the two lives grew impossible to ignore.

The turn

The way home — through my body and the ocean

The change didn't come from understanding it all. I understood plenty. It came when I stopped trying to think my way out and started to work with my body — through somatic regulation, through breath, through the water I already loved. The same ocean that had been my refuge became my teacher: the cold, the buoyancy, the rhythm, all of it speaking directly to a nervous system that had never been allowed to settle. Slowly, in the body, I began to come home to myself.

Now

Why I do this work

The Body of Water is the path I wish I'd had — the tools, the science, the water, and the circle of others I needed when I was finding my own way. I bring my two lives together here: the scientist who understands the living world, and the woman who healed alongside it. If your past is still shaping the life you're living now, I'd be honored to walk part of the way home with you.
