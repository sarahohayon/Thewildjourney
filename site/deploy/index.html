If this is you

You are not broken. You are carrying something.

“Closeness is hard. My relationships keep hurting, and I don't always know why.”

“Strong emotions hijack me — I'm flooded, or numb, and I can't think my way out.”

“The present, and even the future, keep looking like my painful past.”

Nothing is wrong with you. Something happened to you.

Like any mammal facing danger, your body does something wise and automatic — it protects you. Fear, fight, flight, freeze, fawn: these are ancient survival instincts, not flaws. But painful early experiences can leave that alarm switched on, long after the danger has passed. And the parts of the brain that hold the fear fire faster than the parts that think and speak. That is why understanding your story, and talking about it, can only take you so far. The body learned it. The body has to be part of how it heals.

This is a complementary path alongside professional trauma care — never a replacement for it.

Why nature — and why water

The nervous system remembers how to settle. Nature is one of its oldest teachers.

For millions of years, our brains evolved in real jungles — by rivers, along coastlines, in the ocean — not in cities of concrete and glass. Your body is tuned to regulate in the natural world. Returning to it isn't an escape; it's coming back to the conditions you were built for.

A calm, living landscape asks nothing of you — and a nervous system in the presence of something steady begins, quietly, to steady itself. This is regulation through the body, not insight about it.

And water most of all. Cold water on the face triggers a built-in calming reflex within seconds. Being held by the sea — its buoyancy, its rhythm, the sense of being carried — offers the body a rare experience of safety. After ten years of my life spent in and under the water, I have felt this again and again: the ocean is not a backdrop to the healing. It is part of it.

My story

I spent my life healing nature — while quietly struggling to heal myself.

I'm Sarah. I'm a marine and freshwater ecologist and a conservation scientist, and from the outside my life in the water looked like a success. Inside, I carried the anger, shame, and low self-worth of childhood trauma that no achievement could touch — and, for a long time, a fear that it would keep me from the life, and the relationships, I longed for.

The way back wasn't through understanding it all. It was through my body, the ocean, and somatic practice — slowly coming home to myself. That path is what I now walk with other women.

Together, not alone

We were never meant to heal in isolation.

When the people who should have been safe were the danger, closeness itself can feel frightening. That is exactly why we heal in a circle — a nervous system settles most easily in the company of safe others. The community is not an add-on to this work. It is the ground it stands on.
